Thinking in concepts is an approach to understanding and organizing knowledge where one focuses on abstract ideas and principles rather than isolated facts or details. This cognitive strategy involves:

1. Identifying core principles and patterns that underlie specific instances
2. Creating mental frameworks that connect related ideas
3. Using these conceptual structures to analyze new information and situations
4. Recognizing similarities and differences across domains

When [[person - Sarah Paine]] mentioned that she finds thinking in concepts to be very helpful to her work, she was highlighting how this approach allows for more effective analysis and understanding of complex situations. As a historian and strategist, this conceptual thinking enables her to:

- Identify patterns across different historical contexts
- Apply theoretical frameworks to understand geopolitical dynamics
- Make connections between seemingly unrelated events or phenomena
- Communicate complex ideas more effectively

Thinking in concepts is closely related to developing and using [[mental model]], which are cognitive frameworks that help us make sense of the world. Both approaches emphasize creating structured ways of understanding that can be applied across different situations.

The key difference is that mental models are specific frameworks (like supply and demand, evolution, or game theory) while thinking in concepts is the broader cognitive practice of organizing knowledge around abstract ideas rather than concrete details. For a more detailed comparison, see [[diff - thinking in concepts vs. mental models]].

By developing the habit of thinking in concepts, one can more easily:
- Transfer knowledge between domains
- Identify the fundamental principles at work in complex situations
- Communicate more effectively about abstract ideas
- Build a more coherent and useful understanding of the world
